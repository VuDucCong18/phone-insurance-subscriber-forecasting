01 · Business context

Asurion is a leading provider of device insurance, warranty, and support services for cell phones, consumer electronics, and home appliances. With over 300 million devices protected globally, accurate subscriber forecasting is critical to operational and financial planning.

This project forecasts Closing Subs Monthly — the total number of active phone insurance subscribers at the end of each month, broken down by device model, colour, and market. Predicting this one month ahead enables three key decisions:

Claims reserve
Expected claims scale with the active subscriber base — under-reserving creates financial exposure
Parts inventory
Warranty repairs require stocked components per model — wrong forecasts cause stockouts or overstock
Renewal campaigns
Models approaching lifecycle decay need proactive retention campaigns to slow subscriber loss

03 · Data risks & cleaning
Data leakage — what it is and why it matters
We are predicting how many subscribers are active at the end of this month. But Churn (who cancelled) and Filed Claims (who claimed warranty) only become known after the month closes — at the exact same moment as our target. Using them directly as features means the model is trained on information that would not exist in a real forecast scenario. It would look accurate in training but fail in production.
Fix: lag-1 features
Use the previous month's Churn and Filed Claims instead. These are fully known before the forecast period begins — a legitimate leading indicator, not a peek at the answer. The same lag is applied to the prior month's subscriber count, which becomes the strongest honest predictor (r = 0.67).

Pre-order rows dropped — 2,128 rows
Retailers and carriers pre-register subscriber slots in the system before a device officially launches, creating rows where Model Age < 0 — the device hasn't shipped yet. These rows produce corrupt lag values: the "previous month" would be a pre-launch placeholder, not a real subscriber count, poisoning the feature for the first real month of each device.

Justification: 2,128 rows = 16.3% of row count, but only ~4.2% of total subscriber volume. The data integrity gain far outweighs the information loss.
NaN lag rows dropped — 91 rows
The first observed month for each (country, model, colour) group has no prior month to look back at — lag values are undefined. These 91 rows are dropped rather than filled with zero, because zero would be a false signal implying zero prior subscribers, not missing data.

Why a validation set?
The validation set (15%) is used exclusively for hyperparameter tuning — finding the optimal regularisation strength for Ridge and Lasso, and tree depth for Random Forest and XGBoost. The test set is never seen until final evaluation, ensuring that reported metrics are a true, unbiased estimate of real-world performance.
Why TimeSeriesSplit, not K-fold?
Standard K-fold randomly shuffles months across folds — January 2023 could appear in training while December 2022 is in validation, letting future data leak into the past. TimeSeriesSplit enforces strict chronological order: each fold trains only on months before its validation window, exactly mirroring how the model will be used in production.

What MAE of 552 means in practice
XGBoost's MAE of 552 means on average the forecast is off by ±552 subscribers per SKU per month. For iPhone XS Max in California with ~18,000 active subscribers, that is a 3% error — operationally acceptable for claims reserve sizing and parts inventory planning. The error is most relevant on high-volume flagships where operational decisions carry the most financial weight.

Note on MAPE (882%)
MAPE is inflated by low-volume SKUs where actual subscriber counts approach zero — even a small absolute miss becomes a very large percentage. This is a known limitation of MAPE for skewed target distributions. MAE (552) and RMSE (1,006) are the reliable primary metrics here. The right-skewed distribution also means all models are more accurate on high-volume flagship SKUs and proportionally less reliable on niche low-volume devices.
07 · Recommendations
Deploy XGBoost for monthly operational forecasting on existing device fleet
Use as the primary production model for claims reserve sizing, parts inventory planning, and renewal campaign targeting on all currently active SKUs.
Deploy Random Forest for pre-launch forecasting of new device models
When a device has no historical subscriber data, Random Forest's generalizable rules — subscriber stickiness, lifecycle decay, prior month churn signal — produce more reliable estimates than XGBoost's SKU-memorization approach.
Flag low-volume SKUs for manual review
Apply a 500-subscriber threshold: SKUs below this floor should bypass model-driven decisions and go to manual analyst review, as model absolute error can exceed the actual subscriber count.
Short-term: apply log-transform on the target variable
The right-skewed distribution causes all models to over-invest in fitting high-volume flagship outliers. Log-transforming Closing Subs Monthly before training (and exponentiating predictions back) would compress the skew and improve accuracy proportionally across the full SKU range.
Medium-term: integrate SHAP values for per-SKU explainability
SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) would allow analysts to understand exactly why the model forecasts a specific number for a specific device — which features drove the prediction up or down — making results actionable and auditable for business stakeholders.
